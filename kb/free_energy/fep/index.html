Introduction

Free energy perturbation (FEP) is a computational method used in molecular modeling and drug discovery to estimate the free energy difference between two different states of a molecular system. It is based on the concept of thermodynamic equilibrium and uses statistical mechanics principles to calculate the change in free energy as a result of perturbing a system from one state to another.

In FEP, the free energy difference between two states, such as different conformations of a molecule or different ligand-receptor interactions, is calculated by transforming one state into the other through a series of intermediate states using a set of mathematical equations. This involves applying an external perturbation to the system, such as changing the atom positions or modifying the chemical identity of a molecule, and then computing the free energy change associated with this perturbation.

RBFE focuses on estimating the free energy difference between two similar ligands with small chemical modifications, while ABFE estimates the free energy change associated with the complete binding process of a ligand to a protein. RBFE is suitable for studying subtle changes in ligand binding, such as the effects of specific chemical groups, while ABFE provides a broader picture of the overall binding energetics of a ligand-protein complex. Both methods have their strengths and limitations and are used in different contexts depending on the research question and the specific system being studied.

Absolute Binding Free Energy

In ABFE, the ligand itself "disappears" from the binding site, and this process is used to calculate the binding free energy. In the process, the various physical properties, like charges, are slowly turned off on the ligand, so that the receptor effectively stops interacting with the ligand and water can free diffuse in the binding region. This is an "alchemical pathway" that is physically impossible but at the end should generate the same energetic difference from which the binding energy can be calculated.

Relative Binding Free Energy

RBFE involves perturbing the system from one ligand to another by gradually changing the chemical identity of a specific atom, typically referred to as the "perturbation site", while keeping the rest of the system unchanged. This perturbation is done through a series of intermediate states, where the perturbation site is transformed from one ligand to the other, and the free energy change associated with each perturbation step is calculated.

The calculation of RBFE requires careful handling of the perturbation protocol to ensure that the transformation is physically meaningful and that the system remains at thermodynamic equilibrium at each intermediate state. This typically involves carefully selecting the perturbation pathway and choosing appropriate atomistic interactions and force fields to accurately represent the changes in the molecular system.

RBFE can be used to study the effects of specific chemical modifications on the binding affinity of ligands to a protein target, providing valuable insights into the structure-activity relationships (SAR) and guiding the rational design of ligands with improved binding properties. RBFE calculations can also help in understanding the contributions of different molecular interactions, such as hydrogen bonding, van der Waals interactions, and electrostatic interactions, to the overall binding free energy difference between two ligands.

However, RBFE calculations can be computationally demanding and require careful validation against experimental data to ensure accuracy. They also rely on the quality of the force fields and other parameters used in the calculations, which can introduce uncertainties. Despite these challenges, RBFE has been widely used in drug discovery and computational chemistry to provide insights into ligand binding energetics and aid in the rational design of optimized drug candidates.
